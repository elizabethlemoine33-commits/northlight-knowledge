# Evidence

> *The record of what supports our understanding.*

---

## Purpose

Evidence bridges the gap between observation and understanding.

An observation may describe something that happened once.

Evidence demonstrates whether that observation represents a meaningful pattern.

The purpose of collecting evidence is not to prove ourselves right.

It is to increase confidence, challenge assumptions, and strengthen decision-making.

Evidence allows our knowledge to remain grounded in experience rather than opinion.

---

# What Counts as Evidence?

Evidence may come from many sources.

Examples include:

- Client engagements
- Internal projects
- Case studies
- Interviews
- Research papers
- Books
- Industry reports
- Published articles
- Blog posts
- Workshops
- Metrics and analytics
- Surveys
- Experiments
- Historical records

Different forms of evidence carry different strengths.

The objective is not quantity.

It is relevance.

---

# Multiple Sources Matter

Strong ideas rarely depend upon a single source.

Confidence increases when similar patterns emerge across:

- different organisations
- different industries
- different time periods
- different researchers
- different methodologies

Agreement is not required.

Repeated patterns are.

---

# Evidence Is Not Proof

Evidence strengthens confidence.

It does not eliminate uncertainty.

Every piece of evidence should be viewed as contributing to a larger body of knowledge rather than providing a final answer.

New evidence should always have the ability to refine previous conclusions.

# Repository Sources

Evidence may originate from within or outside Northlight.

Internal sources include:

- Advisory engagements
- Operational work
- Product development
- AI experiments
- Workshops
- Client conversations
- Internal documentation

External sources include:

- Academic research
- Books
- Industry publications
- Government guidance
- Public datasets
- Conferences
- Independent research

Neither is inherently superior.

Both contribute to understanding.

---

# Evaluating Evidence

When adding evidence, consider:

**Source**

Where did this originate?

**Reliability**

How trustworthy is the source?

**Repeatability**

Has this pattern appeared elsewhere?

**Recency**

Is the evidence still relevant?

**Context**

What conditions influenced the outcome?

---

# Evidence Is Cumulative

Rarely does one document change our understanding.

Knowledge develops through accumulation.

Small observations, repeated over time, often become stronger than dramatic individual events.

---

# Preserve Contradictions

Contradictory evidence should not be discarded.

Instead, it should be preserved and explored.

Contradictions often reveal:

- hidden assumptions
- contextual differences
- incomplete models
- opportunities for deeper understanding

The repository should become more accurate through disagreement, not less.

---

# Final Thought

Evidence should challenge our thinking as often as it confirms it.

If every new piece of evidence simply reinforces what we already believe, we have probably stopped learning.
