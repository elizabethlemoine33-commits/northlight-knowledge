# Observations

> *The field notes of Northlight.*

---

## Purpose

Observations are the foundation of the Northlight Knowledge repository.

Everything begins here.

An observation records something that has been noticed during practice.

It does not attempt to explain why something happened.

It does not propose a solution.

It simply captures reality as accurately as possible.

Over time, repeated observations may accumulate into evidence, contribute to current understandings, and eventually support core principles.

Without observations, there is no knowledge.

---

# What Is an Observation?

An observation is a statement describing something that has been seen, heard, measured, or experienced.

Good observations answer questions such as:

- What happened?
- Who was involved?
- What changed?
- What repeated?
- What surprised us?
- What pattern appeared?

They avoid speculation.

Instead of asking:

> Why did this happen?

They begin with:

> What actually happened?

---

# Characteristics of Good Observations

Good observations are:

- specific
- descriptive
- objective
- repeatable
- contextual
- dated

They distinguish between facts and interpretation.

---

# Examples

### Strong

> Three department heads independently described the same approval process differently.

---

> Weekly leadership meetings became 40% shorter after decision ownership was clarified.

---

> Employees created personal spreadsheets despite the existence of an official reporting system.

---

### Weak

> Leadership communication is terrible.

---

> The CRM is broken.

---

> Employees don't care.

These statements are conclusions rather than observations.

They should be investigated further before being recorded.

---

# Recording Observations

Each observation should include:

- Date
- Context
- Source
- Observation
- Related Documents
- Initial Confidence

Example:

```markdown
## 2026-07-17

### Context

Fractional COO engagement.

### Observation

Managers consistently delayed decisions that crossed departmental boundaries.

### Source

Leadership workshop.

### Confidence

Medium
```

# Categories

Observations may relate to:

- leadership
- communication
- decision-making
- governance
- organisational design
- operations
- AI adoption
- technology
- knowledge management
- change management
- culture
- customer experience

Categories exist to aid discovery.

They should never constrain observation.

---

# Frequency Matters

One observation is interesting.

Repeated observations become evidence.

The repository should preserve both.

Patterns often emerge only after many observations have accumulated.

---

# Field Notes

Observations do not need to be polished.

Field notes may include:

- sketches
- diagrams
- questions
- partial ideas
- contradictions
- unexpected outcomes
- unfinished thoughts

Capturing imperfect observations today is more valuable than remembering polished ones later.

---

# Repository Relationship

```
Practice
    ↓
Observation
    ↓
Evidence
    ↓
Current Understanding
    ↓
Core Principle
```

Observations should remain linked to the evidence and understandings they eventually support.

The path from observation to principle should always be traceable.

---

# Final Thought

Observe generously.

Interpret cautiously.

Conclude slowly.
